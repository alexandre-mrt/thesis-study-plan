# Proof that the Inverse of a Vandermonde Matrix Is Another Vandermonde Matrix

In the previous chapter on the [[inverse-number-theoretic-transform]], we claimed that the inverse of the Vandermonde matrix $V(\omega)$ for a primitive $k$-th root of unity $\omega$ is also a Vandermonde matrix, given by $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$. We now prove this fact.

*Less mathematically inclined readers can skip this chapter without any loss. It is not necessary to understand the INTT, provided they accept that our proposed inverse matrix is valid.*

The Vandermonde matrix is defined as a matrix where each row is a geometric progression.

- The first row is the geometric progression of $\omega^0$ from $(\omega^0)^0$ to $(\omega)^{k-1}$.
- The second row is the geometric progression of $\omega^1$ from $(\omega^1)^{0}$ to $(\omega^1)^{k -1}$.
- This continues until the last row, which is the geometric progression of $\omega^{k-1}$ from $(\omega^{k-1})^0$ to $(\omega^{k-1})^{k-1}$.

$$
V(\omega)=\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^1)^0  & (\omega^1)^1 & (\omega^1)^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^1)^{k-1} \\ (\omega^2)^0  & (\omega^{2})^1 & (\omega^2)^2 & \cdots & (\omega^2)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{k-1})^0  & (\omega^{k-1})^1 & (\omega^{k-1})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{k-1})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Thus, each entry in row $i$ and column $j$ of $V(\omega)$ is given by

$$
(\omega^i)^j
$$

Consider a polynomial $f(x)$ of degree at most $k-1$, given by

$$
f(x)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots+ a_{k-1}x^{k-1}
$$

Let $S$ be the set consisting of the $k$-th roots of unity generated by a primitive $k$-th root:

$$
S=\{\omega^0, \omega^1, \omega^2, \cdots \omega^{k-1}\}
$$

The vector of evaluations of $f(x)$ on the set $S$,

$$
\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 
f(\omega^0) \\ f(\omega^1) \\ f(\omega^2) \\ \vdots \\ f(\omega^{k-1}) 
\end{bmatrix}
$$

can be obtained by multiplying the coefficient vector $\mathbf{a}$ by $V(\omega)$:

$$
\mathbf{y}= V(\omega) \cdot \mathbf{a}\
$$

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 
f(\omega^0) \\ f(\omega^1) \\ f(\omega^2) \\ \vdots \\ f(\omega^{k-1}) 
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^1)^0  & (\omega^1)^1 & (\omega^1)^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^1)^{k-1} \\ (\omega^2)^0  & (\omega^{2})^1 & (\omega^2)^2 & \cdots & (\omega^2)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{k-1})^0  & (\omega^{k-1})^1 & (\omega^{k-1})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{k-1})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
a_0 \\ a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_{k-1}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

where $a_0, a_1, ..., a_{k-1}$ are the coefficients of the polynomial $f(x)$.

Each row on the left-hand side is the inner product of the $i$-th row of the Vandermonde matrix and the vector $\mathbf{a}$. For instance, the $i$-th element $f(\omega^i)$ is

$$
\begin{aligned}
f(\omega^i) &= \langle [(\omega^i)^0,(\omega^i)^1, \ldots, (\omega^i)^{k-1}],\mathbf{a} \rangle \\
&= (\omega^i)^0 a_0 + (\omega^i)^1a_1 + \ldots + (\omega^i)^{k-1} a_{k-1} \\
&= \omega^{i0} a_0 \phantom{()} + \omega^{i1}a_1 \phantom{()} + \ldots + \omega^{i(k-1)} a_{k-1}
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, the evaluations can be written as

$$
f(\omega^i) = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{ij} a_j
$$

for $i\in \{0,1,2,...,k-1\}$.

*Note on indices: In the equation above, we have two indices. The index $i$ indicates which evaluation we are dealing with, and it can take the values $0,1,2,\ldots, k-1$. This means that the formula above represents not just one equation, but $k$ equations, one for each value of $i$. For instance, the notation above is a shorthand for*

$$
\begin{aligned}
f(\omega^0) &= \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{0j} a_j \\
f(\omega^1) &= \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{1j} a_j \\
\vdots \\
f(\omega^{k-1}) &= \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{(k-1)j} a_j \\
\end{aligned}
$$

*The index $j$ in $\omega^{ij}$ and $a_j$ is a summation index and therefore “consumed” by the sum. Because it is consumed by the sum, it does not appear on the left-hand side of the equation. The choice of the indices $i$ and $j$ is arbitrary; we could have used $m,n,p,q$ or any other letters. In general, for vector indices, it is common to use letters from the middle of the alphabet.*

Now we want to find the inverse relation to obtain the vector $\mathbf{a}$ from the vector $\mathbf{y}$. In other words, we want to calculate $\mathbf{a} = V^{-1}(\omega) \cdot \mathbf{y}$, where $V^{-1}(\omega)$ is the inverse of $V(\omega)$.

Our claim is that the inverse of the Vandermonde matrix $V(\omega)$ is also a Vandermonde matrix, given by $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$.

To be completely clear, our claim is that

$$
V^{-1}(\omega) = \frac{1}{k} V({\omega^{-1}})
$$

where $\omega$ is a primitive $k$-th root of unity.

If the claim is correct, the vector $\mathbf{a}$ can be calculated as follows:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1}) \cdot \mathbf{y}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}\begin{bmatrix}a_0 \\a_1 \\\vdots \\a_i \\\vdots \\a_{k-1}\end{bmatrix}&=\frac{1}{k}\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1} \\(\omega^{-1})^0 & (\omega^{-1})^1 & \cdots & (\omega^{-1})^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\(\omega^{-i})^0 & (\omega^{-i})^1 & \cdots & (\omega^{-i})^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\(\omega^{-(k-1)})^0 & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^1 & \cdots & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^{k-1}\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}f(\omega^0) \\f(\omega^1) \\\vdots \\f(\omega^{k-1})\end{bmatrix}\end{aligned}
$$

The component $a_i$ is the inner product between the $i$-th row of $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ and the vector $\mathbf{y}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
a_i
&=
\frac{1}{k}
\begin{bmatrix}
(\omega^{-i})^0 & (\omega^{-i})^1 & \cdots & (\omega^{-i})^{k-1}
\end{bmatrix} \cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
f(\omega^0) \\
f(\omega^1) \\
\vdots \\
f(\omega^{k-1})
\end{bmatrix} \\
&=\frac{1}{k}(\omega^{-i})^0 f(\omega^0) + \frac{1}{k}(\omega^{-i})^1 f(\omega^1) + \ldots + \frac{1}{k}(\omega^{-i})^{k-1} f(\omega^{k-1}) \\&=\frac{1}{k}\omega^{-i0} f(\omega^0)\phantom{()} + \frac{1}{k}\omega^{-i1} f(\omega^1)\phantom{()} + \ldots + \frac{1}{k}\omega^{-i(k-1)} f(\omega^{k-1})
\end{aligned}
$$

This operation can be written as the following sum:

$$
\begin{aligned}
a_i &= \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{k} \omega^{-im} f(\omega^m)
\end{aligned}
$$

for $i\in\{0,1,2,...,k-1\}$.

For pedagogical reasons, we will prove our claim - that the inverse of the Vandermonde matrix $V(\omega)$ is another Vandermonde matrix, given by $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ - in two different ways.

First, we will show that the matrix multiplication of $V(\omega)$ and $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ yields the [identity matrix](https://www.notion.so/Proof-that-the-Inverse-of-a-Vandermonde-Matrix-Is-Another-Vandermonde-Matrix-2c409cb3e96280cbaec0d6e365b4c245?pvs=21) $\mathbb{I}$, that is,

$$
V(\omega) \cdot \frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1}) = \mathbb{I}
$$

Then, we will show that if we first multiply $V(\omega)$ by $\mathbf{a}$ to obtain $\mathbf{y}$, and then multiply $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ by $V(\omega)\mathbf{a}$, we recover $\mathbf{a}$.

These two proofs are essentially the same, just expressed in two different ways.

## Proof that $V(\omega) \cdot \frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1}) = \mathbb{I}$

We will prove that the matrix multiplication of $V(\omega)$ with $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ yields the identity matrix.

The proof relies on the [[orthogonality-of-roots-of-unity|orthogonality property]] of the roots of unity, which is expressed as a summation. Therefore, we first write the matrix multiplication in summation form so that we can use this orthogonality property.

### The multiplication $V(\omega) \cdot \frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ in index notation

Recall that the matrix entry of $V(\omega)$ in row $i$ and column $m$ is given by

$$
\omega^{im}
$$

*Note: The numbering of rows and columns here is considered from $0$, not $1$.*

For example, the entry in row $1$ and column $2$ of $V(\omega)$ is $(\omega^1)^2,$ as highlighted below in red:

$$
V(\omega) 
 = \begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^1)^0  & (\omega^1)^1 & \color{red}(\omega^1)^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^1)^{k-1} \\ (\omega^2)^0  & (\omega^{2})^1 & (\omega^2)^2 & \cdots & (\omega^2)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{k-1})^0  & (\omega^{k-1})^1 & (\omega^{k-1})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{k-1})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
$$

Also, the matrix entry of $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ in row $m$ and column $j$ is given by

$$
\frac{1}{k} \omega^{-mj}
$$

For example, the entry in row $2$ and column $0$ of $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ is $(\omega^{-2})^0$, as highlighted below:

$$
\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1}) =  \frac{1}{k}\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^{-1})^0  & (\omega^{-1})^1 & (\omega^{-1})^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^{-1})^{k-1} \\ \color{red}(\omega^{-2})^0  & (\omega^{-2})^1 & (\omega^{-2})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{-2})^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{-(k-1)})^0  & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^1 & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
$$

Therefore, when the two matrices $V(\omega)$ and $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ are multiplied, the resulting element in row $i$ and column $j$ is obtained by multiplying row $i$ of $V(\omega)$ (red) with column $j$ of $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ (blue):

$$
V(\omega)\cdot \frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1}) =  
\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^1)^0  & (\omega^1)^1 & (\omega^1)^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^1)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\  \color{red}(\omega^i)^0  & \color{red}(\omega^{i})^1 & \color{red}(\omega^i)^2 & \color{red}\cdots & \color{red}(\omega^i)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{k-1})^0  & (\omega^{k-1})^1 & (\omega^{k-1})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{k-1})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
\frac{1}{k}\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & \cdots \color{blue}(\omega^0)^j  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^{-1})^0  & (\omega^{-1})^1 & \cdots \color{blue}(\omega^{-1})^{j} & \cdots & (\omega^{-1})^{k-1} \\ (\omega^{-2})^0  & (\omega^{-2})^1 & \cdots \color{blue}(\omega^{-2})^j & \cdots & (\omega^{-2})^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \color{blue}\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{-(k-1)})^0  & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^1 & \cdots \color{blue}(\omega^{-(k-1)})^j & \cdots & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
$$

Each entry of the matrix $V(\omega) \cdot \frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$, which we denote by $M_{ij}$, is then given by

$$
\begin{aligned}M_{ij} &= \color{red}{(\omega^i)^0}\,\color{blue}{(\omega^0)^j} + \color{red}{(\omega^i)^1}\,\color{blue}{(\omega^{-1})^j} + \cdots + \color{red}{(\omega^i)^{k-1}}\,\color{blue}{(\omega^{-(k-1)})^j}\\[6pt]&= \color{red}{\omega^{i0}}\,\color{blue}{\omega^{0j}} + \color{red}{\omega^{i1}}\,\color{blue}{\omega^{-1j}} + \cdots + \color{red}{\omega^{i(k-1)}}\,\color{blue}{\omega^{-(k-1)j}}\\[6pt]&= \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \color{red}{\omega^{im}}\,\frac{1}{k}\color{blue}{\omega^{-mj}}\\[6pt]&= \frac{1}{k}\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j}\end{aligned}
$$

Now we need to show that the entries $M_{ij}$ are equal to those of the identity matrix. To do this, we use the orthogonality of the roots of unity.

### Recall: Orthogonality of roots of unity

In the chapter on the orthogonality of roots of unity, we showed that

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{i-j}=\begin{cases}k, & \text{if } i\equiv j \pmod{k},\\[6pt]0, & \text{otherwise}.\end{cases}
$$

To recap, the formula above gives us the result of the summation in two cases: (1) when $i\equiv j$ and (2) when $i \not\equiv j$. We will use both cases below.

### Case 1: When $i\equiv j$

We want to calculate

$$
M_{ij} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j}
$$

when $i\equiv j$. The orthogonality of the roots of unity says that, when $i\equiv j$,

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j} = k
$$

Using this result for $M_{ij}$, we have that

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_{ij} &= \frac{1}{k} \boxed{\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j}} \\
&= \frac{1}{k} k = 1
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, for the diagonal terms (such as $M_{00}, M_{11}, M_{22},...,M_{(k-1)(k-1)}$), we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_{ij} &= 1 && \text{when }i = j
\end{aligned}
$$

### Case 2: When $i \not\equiv j$

Now we want to calculate

$$
M_{ij} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j}
$$

when $i\not\equiv j$. The orthogonality of the roots of unity says that, when $i\not\equiv j$ ,

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j} = 0
$$

Using this result for $M_{ij}$, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_{ij} &= \frac{1}{k} \boxed{\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{\,i-j}} \\
&= \frac{1}{k} 0 = 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore, for any non-diagonal terms (such as $M_{10}, M_{01}, M_{12}, \dots)$, we have $M_{ij} = 0$.

### The matrix $M$

Taking into account cases (1) and (2) above, we have that the matrix $M = (M_{ij})$ is

$$
M =\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\0 & 0 & \cdots & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$

which is exactly the identity matrix $\mathbb{I}$. Thus, the matrices $V(\omega)$ and $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ are [inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix) of each other, because

$$
V(\omega)  \cdot \left(\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})\right) = M = \mathbb{I}
$$

## Matrix multiplication of $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ with vector $\mathbf{y}$ yields vector $\mathbf{a}$ back

Another way to show that $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ is the inverse of $V(\omega)$ is to show that it inverts the latter.

That is, if we first apply $V(\omega)$ to $\mathbf{a}$ to obtain $\mathbf{y}$,

$$
\mathbf{y} = V(\omega)\cdot \mathbf{a}
$$

and then apply $\frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1})$ to $\mathbf{y}$, we recover $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1}) \cdot \mathbf{y}
$$

That is exactly what we will show.

### First transformation: $\mathbf{y} = V(\omega) \cdot \mathbf{a}$

The first transformation takes the vector of coefficients $\mathbf{a}$ to the vector of evaluations $\mathbf{y}$ via the following matrix operation:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 
f(\omega^0) \\ f(\omega^1) \\ f(\omega^2) \\ \vdots \\ f(\omega^{k-1}) 
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^1)^0  & (\omega^1)^1 & (\omega^1)^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^1)^{k-1} \\ (\omega^2)^0  & (\omega^{2})^1 & (\omega^2)^2 & \cdots & (\omega^2)^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{k-1})^0  & (\omega^{k-1})^1 & (\omega^{k-1})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{k-1})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
a_0 \\ a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_{k-1}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

As we have already seen, this matrix operation can be written using indices:

$$
f(\omega^i) = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{ij} a_j
$$

### Second transformation: $\mathbf{\tilde{a}} = \frac{1}{k}V(\omega^{-1}) \mathbf{y}$

Now we perform a second transformation on the vector $\mathbf{y}$, which results in a vector $\mathbf{\tilde{a}}$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\tilde{a}_0 \\ \tilde{a}_1 \\ \tilde{a}_2 \\\vdots \\ \tilde{a}_{k-1}
\end{bmatrix}= \frac{1}{k}\begin{bmatrix}(\omega^0)^0 & (\omega^0)^1 & (\omega^0)^2  & \cdots & (\omega^0)^{k-1}  \\(\omega^{-1})^0  & (\omega^{-1})^1 & (\omega^{-1})^{2} & \cdots & (\omega^{-1})^{k-1} \\ (\omega^{-2})^0  & (\omega^{-2})^1 & (\omega^{-2})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{-2})^{k-1} \\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (\omega^{-(k-1)})^0  & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^1 & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^2 & \cdots & (\omega^{-(k-1)})^{k-1} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
f(\omega^0) \\ f(\omega^1) \\ f(\omega^2) \\\vdots \\ f(\omega^{k-1})
\end{bmatrix}
$$

If we can show that $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{\tilde{a}}$, then the second transformation is the inverse of the first.

The matrix operation above can be written in index form as

$$
\tilde{a}_j = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{k} \omega^{-jm} f(\omega^m)
$$

Now substitute the expression for $f(\omega^i)$ . Recall that

$$
f(\omega^i) = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \omega^{ij} a_j
$$

where $i$ is just an index that we can replace for $m$, or any other letter. Thus, replacing $f(\omega^m)$ in the result for $\tilde{a}_j$, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{a}_j &= \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{k} \omega^{-jm} f(\omega^m) \\
&= \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{k} \omega^{-jm} \left(\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \omega^{mi} a_i \right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Rewriting as a double sum:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{a}_j &= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \sum_{m=0}^{k-1}  \frac{1}{k} \omega^{-jm}  \omega^{mi} a_i \\
&= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}  \frac{1}{k} \left( \sum_{m=0}^{k-1}  \omega^{m(i-j)} \right) a_i
\end{aligned}
$$

The term in parentheses is exactly the orthogonality relation:

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{i-j}=\begin{cases}k, & \text{if } i\equiv j \pmod{k},\\[6pt]0, & \text{otherwise}.\end{cases}
$$

To continue the proof, we could study case (1), where $i=j$, and case (2), where $i \neq j$, but we will proceed differently. We will introduce a symbol called the **Kronecker delta.**

The Kronecker delta, $\delta_{ij}$, is a symbol with 2 indices, $i$ and $j$ in this case, which is $1$ when $i=j$ and $0$ otherwise:

$$
\delta_{ij} =\begin{cases}1 & \text{if } i = j \\0 & \text{if } i \ne j\end{cases}
$$

The Kronecker delta can be understood as the entries of the identity matrix.

Using the Kronecker delta, we can write the orthogonality property as

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{k-1} (\omega^{m})^{i-j} = k \delta_{ij}
$$

Using the Kronecker delta in the expression for $\tilde{a}_j$, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{a}_j &= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}  \frac{1}{k} \left( \sum_{m=0}^{k-1}  \omega^{m(i-j)} \right) a_i \\
&= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}  \frac{1}{k} \left( k\delta_{ij} \right) a_i \\
&= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}  \frac{1}{\cancel{k}} \left( \cancel{k}\delta_{ij} \right) a_i \\
&= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}  \delta_{ij} a_i 
\end{aligned}
$$

Expanding the summation, the expression above can be written as

$$
\tilde{a}_j = \delta_{0j} a_0 + \delta_{1j} a_1 + \delta_{2j} a_2 + ... + \delta_{(k-1)j}a_{k-1}
$$

By the Kronecker delta property, only one term in the summation is nonzero. For example, for $j=2$, only $\delta_{22}$ is nonzero. Thus, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{a}_2
&= \delta_{02} a_0 + \delta_{12} a_1 + \delta_{22} a_2 + \cdots + \delta_{(k-1)2} a_{k-1} \\
&= \cancel{\delta_{02} a_0} + \cancel{\delta_{12} a_1} + \delta_{22} a_2 + \cdots + \cancel{\delta_{(k-1)2} a_{k-1}} \\
&= a_2
\end{aligned}
$$

This holds true for any $j$, therefore we have that

$$
\tilde{a}_j = a_j
$$

for any $j$.

This shows that the transformation $\frac{1}{k} V(\omega^{-1})$ is the inverse of the transformation $V(\omega)$.
