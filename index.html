Methodology

How to Read & Learn Efficiently

Evidence-based techniques tailored to your brain

Your Brain’s Two Modes

Key insight: Your brain alternates between focused mode (intense concentration) and diffuse mode (relaxed background processing). You already know your brain works well with pauses — the science confirms it. The trick is to design your study sessions around both modes deliberately.
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ YOUR LEARNING CYCLE │ ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤ │ │ │ ┌───────────┐ ┌───────────┐ ┌───────────┐ │ │ │ FOCUSED │ ──→ │ BREAK │ ──→ │ DIFFUSE │ │ │ │ MODE │ │ 5-10min │ │ MODE │ │ │ └───────────┘ └───────────┘ └─────┬─────┘ │ │ 25 min deep Walk, stretch, │ │ │ reading no screens │ Background │ │ │ connections │ │ ┌───────────┐ │ │ │ │ RECALL │ ←─────────────────────────┘ │ │ │ & TEST │ After break, test yourself │ │ └───────────┘ before re-reading │ │ │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Source: Barbara Oakley, Learning How to Learn (Coursera, 40M+ enrolled). The “incubation effect” is well-documented in cognitive science — your diffuse mode builds neural connections that focused mode alone cannot.

The Three-Pass Method (Keshav)

The gold standard for reading research papers. From S. Keshav’s widely-cited “How to Read a Paper” (ACM SIGCOMM). Do NOT read linearly. Each pass has a specific goal.
1
Bird’s Eye View
5–10 minutes
  • Read title, abstract, introduction
  • Read section headings only
  • Glance at figures and their captions
  • Read conclusion
  • Scan references (which ones do you already know?)
After Pass 1, answer the 5 C’s:
Category • Context • Correctness • Contributions • Clarity
If the paper isn’t relevant, stop here. No guilt.
2
Detailed Read
~1 hour (with breaks)
  • Read carefully but skip proofs/complex math
  • Study every figure, diagram, and table
  • Annotate key claims in the margin
  • Note unread references to follow up
  • Write a 3-sentence summary from memory
Your brain tip: Take a 10-min walk after Pass 2. Let diffuse mode integrate what you read. Then write the summary without looking at the paper.
3
Deep Reconstruction
4+ hours (only for core papers)
  • Mentally reconstruct the paper from scratch
  • Work through proofs and math line-by-line
  • Identify hidden assumptions
  • Compare construction with alternatives
  • Ask: “How would I improve this?”
Only do Pass 3 for “core” papers. For your thesis, that’s ~15 of 44 papers. The rest stop at Pass 1 or 2. This is not laziness — it’s strategic allocation.

Source: S. Keshav, “How to Read a Paper” (ACM SIGCOMM, 2007). Cross-referenced with “Ten simple rules for reading a scientific paper” (PLoS Computational Biology, 2020).

Your Optimized Study Session

Designed for your brain. You process well with pauses and background integration. Here’s the protocol, combining Pomodoro timing + spaced retrieval + diffuse mode breaks.
25 min
FOCUS
Pass 1 of 2–3 papers

Skim title, abstract, headings, figures, conclusion. Use the 5 C’s to decide: skip, pass 2, or core (pass 3 later). Mark status in the study plan.

5 min
BREAK
Walk, stretch, look out the window. No screens. Your brain is connecting the dots.
25 min
FOCUS
Pass 2 of one paper

Read carefully. Study figures. Annotate claims. Note which referenced papers you need. Skip proofs for now.

10 min
DIFFUSE
Longer break. Walk outside if possible. Your brain is now building the mental model. This is when the “aha” moments happen.
15 min
RECALL
Active recall test (without looking at the paper)

Write from memory: (1) What problem does the paper solve? (2) What is the core construction? (3) How does it connect to your thesis? Then check against the paper. The gaps reveal what you actually learned.

25 min
FOCUS
Interleave: switch to a different chapter

If you just read a Ch 2.1 paper (anonymous credentials), now read a Ch 2.2 paper (range proofs) or Ch 2.3 (TEE). Interleaving forces your brain to compare and contrast, building deeper connections between thesis chapters.

Why this works for you: The 25+5+25+10+15+25 cycle (~2 hours) gives your brain 3 focused blocks with 2 diffuse breaks and 1 recall block. The interleaving in block 4 forces cross-chapter connections (e.g., “how do Bulletproofs++ range proofs interact with BBS+ credential proofs?”). After 2 hours, take a real 30-min break. Your brain will keep processing.

Sources: Kang (2016), “Spaced Repetition Promotes Efficient and Effective Learning” • Investigating Break-Taking Techniques (2025) • Sana & Yan (2022), Interleaved Retrieval Practice

Paper Triage: Your 44 Papers, Strategically

Not all papers deserve the same effort. Use the relevance badges in the study plan to guide your depth.
CORE ~15 papers

Pass 1 + 2 + 3. Full reconstruction. Work through the math. Implement a toy version if possible. These are the papers your thesis builds on directly.

RELATED ~15 papers

Pass 1 + 2. Understand the contribution and how it relates. No need for proof reconstruction. Cite them, know what they do, move on.

CONTEXT ~14 papers

Pass 1 only. Skim abstract + conclusion. Know they exist. Reference for completeness. 5 minutes max per paper.

Time estimate: Context papers (14 × 10 min) = ~2.5 hours • Related papers (15 × 1.5 hours) = ~22 hours • Core papers (15 × 6 hours) = ~90 hours
Total: ~115 hours of focused reading over 4–5 months before thesis starts. That’s about 6–7 hours per week.

Retention Techniques

1. Active Recall (not re-reading)

After reading a paper, close it. Write down: What was the main contribution? What technique did they use? How does it connect to my thesis? Only then re-open to check. Research shows recall doubles retention vs. re-reading (Karpicke & Blunt, 2011).

2. Spaced Repetition

Review paper summaries at expanding intervals: 1 day → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks → 1 month. The paper recap cards on this site are your review material. Revisit the “Technical” view of each paper card on the spaced schedule.

3. Interleaving (mix chapters)

Never read 3 papers from the same chapter in a row. Alternate: BBS+ (Ch 2.1) → Bulletproofs++ (Ch 2.2) → TEE.Fail (Ch 2.3). This feels harder but produces 43% better retention in controlled studies (Rohrer & Taylor, 2007).

4. Explain to Someone (or Rubber Duck)

Use the “Intuitive” view of each paper card as inspiration: can you explain the paper’s core idea using the analogy? If not, you don’t understand it yet. The Feynman technique: teach it simply, find the gaps, go back and learn those gaps.

5. Sleep On It (literally)

Read a challenging paper in the evening. Sleep. Review your notes the next morning. Sleep consolidates procedural and declarative memory (Walker, 2008). Your brain literally replays and strengthens the neural pathways formed during reading.

Sources: Evidence of the Spacing Effect (PMC, 2022) • SQ3R / Learning Scientists • Science Magazine — How to seriously read a paper
